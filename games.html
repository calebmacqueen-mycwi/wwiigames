Axis and Allies

Axis and Allies is an amazing board game. When playing, you control one or more of the major powers from World War II. Such as the United States, the United Kingdom, the USSR, Germany, or Japan. The game plays sort of like Risk, but with tons more depth, such as having each unit (such as tanks, infantry, and artillery) fight differently. Because of the increased depth, be prepared for an extended game, that can last for an entire day.

Your goal is to work with your allies to either take control of your opponent's capital cities, or beat them into submission.

Axis and Allies Miniatures

This World War II miniatures game has been out of print for a while, but remains fun to play. The game focuses on small-scale engagements, typically between a couple tanks and small squads of infantry. The game has a wide variety of units, ranging from Russian conscripts to King Tigers.

War at Sea

This miniatures game is very similar to Axis and Allies miniatures, but take place at sea (After all, they were made by the same company). The game works really well, but we feel like the recommmended 100 point limit for fleets is too small, so we often play 200 point games.

The reason that a 100 point game feels so small is that in order to both preserve game balance, along with a sense of realism, battleships cost well over 50 points, and even going as high as 80 points. Aircraft carriers are not much better. Although you only pay about 20 points for the carrier, you have to pay 30-50 more points to put planes on it. All of these issues are irrelevant if you just give each player some extra points to work with. The only downside is that games take longer.

Company of Heroes 2

This real-time strategy game incorporates some really cool features that I haven't seen in any other game. The economy is directly tied in to map control, so you do not have to constantly go back to your base to make sure you are gathering the right recources. This frees you up to focus on keeping your tanks alive (very difficult for the Americans. Shermans are not known for their armor).

Second, in most real-time strategy games units can see 360 degrees around them, regardless of what happens to be in the way. Company of Heroes fixes this with what is called the True-Sight system. Most units can still see 360 degrees, but objects, like houses, smoke, and fences block line of sight. This makes avoiding ambushes a lot harder.
